## Notes

### Junior Security Analyst Intro

#### Your Daily Duties

As a Junior Security Analyst, also called a Level 1 Analyst, you typically work as part of a 24/7 security team where your main responsibility is reviewing and triaging security alerts alongside your colleagues. Day to day, this means monitoring and investigating alerts, taking part in brainstorms and workshops, cooperating with other teams to help protect the organization, and continuously building your knowledge so you can recognize new attack techniques and understand the defenses used against them.

### SOC Role in Blue Team

#### Your Daily Duties

As you gain experience in a SOC, you begin to see how junior analyst work builds the foundation for more advanced roles across cyber security. Your daily duties may include detecting and preventing threats such as data stealers on employee devices, analyzing and helping stop phishing campaigns aimed at specific departments like finance, contributing during major incidents such as ransomware attacks, and collaborating with teammates to create better detection rules and automations. Over time, you also need to understand how the business itself operates so your security work supports real organizational needs.

### Humans as Attack Vectors

This room focused on attacks that target people, often considered the weakest element in cyber security. It explained why attackers use social engineering and similar techniques against humans, and how a security analyst can help detect and prevent these threats. A key takeaway is that this area changes constantly, so staying current with new attack methods and trends is part of the job.

Types of attacks:

- Phishing, where attackers trick users with fraudulent emails or messages.
- Spear phishing, which targets a specific person or team with tailored content.
- Vishing and smishing, which use phone calls or text messages to manipulate victims.
- Malware download lures, where users are persuaded to open attachments, install fake updates, or download seemingly legitimate files that contain malicious code.
- AI-assisted deepfakes, where attackers use generated voice, video, or text to impersonate trusted people and make scams or fraudulent requests appear convincing.
- Pretexting, where an attacker invents a believable story to gain trust or sensitive information.
- Baiting, where victims are lured into unsafe actions by curiosity or false promises.
- Tailgating and impersonation, where attackers exploit trust to gain physical or logical access.

### Systems as Attack Vectors

This room focused on how attackers abuse weaknesses in systems rather than directly manipulating people. Even though analysts do not typically manage systems directly, understanding the common attacks and defenses, and sharing them with the IT department, is a key part of broadening your cyber security perspective. If you want to grow quickly and be a strong team player, stay updated on the latest threats and always share the news with others.

Common system-focused attack areas:

- Human-led attacks, where an attacker gains interactive access and then manually moves through an environment to escalate privileges, evade detections, or reach sensitive systems.
- Vulnerabilities, where attackers exploit software flaws in operating systems, applications, appliances, or services to gain code execution, access data, or disrupt operations.
- Supply chain attacks, where a trusted vendor, software package, update mechanism, or third-party service is compromised so the attacker can reach many downstream victims.
- Misconfigurations, where weak settings such as exposed services, overly broad permissions, default credentials, or missing security controls create opportunities for compromise.
